Section 1
United States Military Academy – Admissions Tips
Last updated June 2026

The purpose of this website is to provide personal advice and insight into the admissions process that should prove useful in your journey to earn a nomination and eventually an appointment. This information is not in lieu of other materials — it is only meant to provide background to many of the questions that Field Force (FF) gets throughout the admissions calendar.

Keys to Success

  • Be proactive throughout the process
  • Your contact details on file – not parents'
  • Apply for all eligible nominations early
  • Prepare, train, & practice for requirements (CFA, SAT &/or ACT); keep testing until your scores plateau
  • Communicate promptly and politely with Field Force
  • Proactiveness and early completion of application reflects drive to attend West Point
  • Consider all college and service ROTC options as backup – especially Army
  • Make the decision that is right for you
  • How to get your file to the top of the pile: westpointadmissions.com blog post

Main Drivers of the Whole Candidate Score (WCS)

Academic

  • Percentile super-scored test scores on ACT and/or SAT (30% of total WCS); top candidates have scores in the 90+ percentiles
  • Top 20% of high school class is a strong indicator of competitiveness (GPA used, not class rank)
  • Depth and breadth of rigorous college prep courses with some AP (scores of 3, 4, & 5), IB, or Concurrent Enrollment background
  • Meeting minimums does not denote competitiveness — top candidates have 4 years each of Math, English, Science (2 years Lab: Chemistry & Physics), History/Social Sciences, plus 2+ years of a foreign language
  • School Official Evaluations (SOEs) — must be current or former teachers of that particular subject
  • National Honor Society membership — 12th grade minimum, 11th grade too if possible

Leadership

  • Extracurricular, club, and community participation — quality, impactful leadership roles over quantity
  • Active participation in roles or positions where you were selected by peers or adult supervision
  • Community service, volunteering, or paid employment

Fitness

  • Participation and lettering in organized athletics, preferably a team sport (school, club, and/or community)
  • Performance on Candidate Fitness Assessment — strive for averages, not minimums
  • Athletic weakness can be greatly overcome by earning above-average CFA scores

Score Enhancers

  • Being in the top 10% of class
  • 90+ percentile super-scored SAT and/or ACT results
  • One or more Concurrent Enrollment university or community college courses
  • Performing, Fine, & Visual Arts participation & leadership (1st chair, drum major, lead in a play, etc.)
  • Captain/Lead/Coach/Mentor of sports or academic team (Debate, Model UN, Robotics, etc.)
  • Training Certificates: EMT, Lifeguard, Ski Patrol, Police Cadet, etc.
  • Participation and Leadership in Student Government, Scouting, Boys/Girls State, JROTC (CAP, Sea Cadets, etc.)
  • Participation and Leadership in FFA, 4H, or other farm/ag/rural focused activities
  • Local, regional, state, or national awards or recognition in anything
  • 1–2 years of college with ROTC participation or military Junior College attendance
  • Above-average scores on the Candidate Fitness Assessment

General Overview

  • Be proactive and aggressive throughout the process. Check email regularly. The speed and quality of FF support is directly proportional to the speed and diligence with which you complete your file. If attending West Point is a priority, show it by your actions and complete your file by the end of October or sooner with a passed CFA on file.
  • Communicate with your assigned FF Representative. Your FF Rep is your first and best point of contact. How you work and communicate is part of the formal evaluation. Establish a professional email address and monitor it regularly.
  • Emails/calls/texts from candidates, not parents. Always include your phone number in your signature block. Do not use a school email account since those expire. Do not respond directly to "Do Not Reply" generic email blasts — start a new email chain.
  • Apply to all available nomination sources (June–August) EARLY! All nominating authorities have separate processes. Most start in summer and end with interviews in October–November.
  • Complete your WP admissions file EARLY — certainly by 31 October or earlier. The earlier you complete your file, the earlier you can be scheduled for Medical and the more clarity you will have. If you wait, you are not a priority.
  • Schedule medical exam PROMPTLY once you receive your first email notification from DODMERB — this is a totally separate process apart from your WP application. Do NOT send medical information in your essays or include it as attachments in your WP portal.
  • Take both ACT and SAT two or more times each — keep testing until your scores plateau. Test scores are the single most important aspect of your WCS. Your portal will not accept scores past the 31 January deadline.
  • Train/Practice the CFA early — preferably by end of October. Many FF Reps do not formally interview candidates unless they see a passed CFA in the file. There are NO waivers for this requirement.
  • Consider college ROTC as a backup. Army ROTC gives out twice as many scholarships as Navy and Air Force and allows ANY major. If you are competitive for West Point, you are extremely competitive for 3 or 4-year Army ROTC Scholarship deals.
  • Be careful with social media. The profession of arms has a long tradition of being apolitical. Offers have been rescinded due to past social media posts.
  • If interested in becoming a recruited athlete: westpoint.edu/admissions/recruited-athletes

Your West Point Admissions File

  • Once you receive your Candidate Checklist (around 1 June), complete your file as promptly as possible — you don't have to wait until June to begin drafting pieces like your resume.
  • Two items you can complete immediately after 1 June without relying on anyone else: the CPDR and the essays/writing prompts. With these plus your 6th Semester Transcript and competitive test scores, Admissions can order your Medical process.
  • Pay particular attention to Candidate Statements/Essays/Writing Prompts. Begin drafting early and complete by end of summer. Be concise (character limits apply), have a genuine and unique story, and keep the BS and weasel words to a minimum.
  • School Official Evaluations (SOEs) — Math, English, & Physical Science SOEs MUST be teachers you currently have or had for that subject. PE SOE is no longer required.
  • Have your school send in Official 6th and 7th semester transcripts when grades are completed.
  • Expect "Candidate Personal Data Record Received" and "Candidate Activities Report Received" by end of summer or first thing when senior year starts. You can continue to update past 31 January as long as WP keeps your file open by emailing: admissionssw@westpoint.edu

Candidate Fitness Assessment (CFA)

  • CFA instructions: westpoint.edu – CFA Instructions
  • Train throughout the summer. Take it before the end of summer. Fail one event and you fail the entire CFA.
  • Candidates struggle most with the basketball throw, pull-ups, and push-ups. For push-ups, make sure chest gets close enough to the ground that the elbow slightly breaks the 90-degree angle on the downward movement; elbows must lock at the top.
  • West Point pull-ups require holding at the top to your adam's apple/bottom of the neck. Passing: 1 for females, 4 for males.
  • Your administrator can conduct your CFA for more than one academy — make sure you have the WP CFA form; take a photo of your results.
  • There are NO waivers for the CFA requirement.

Medical

  • DoDMERB website: dodmerb.tricare.osd.mil — Main number: 719-333-3562 | usaf.dodmerb.helpdesk@mail.mil
  • Study DOD Instruction 6130.03, Volume I for standards.
  • Major disqualifying conditions: previous head/neck injuries, orthopedic corrections/surgeries, food allergies, asthma, circulatory conditions, and vision not correctable to 20/20. Conditions requiring ongoing medication will be greatly scrutinized.
  • Medical waivers are only requested on competitive candidates.
  • Do NOT send medical information to FF or post anything medical-related to your WP admissions portal.

Earning a Nomination

  • Apply for at least 3–4 nominations: your House Representative, both Senators, and the Vice President. You must receive at least one nomination to be eligible for an appointment.
  • Start early. This is a totally separate process from your WP application. Make it your goal to have all nomination applications finished before your senior year starts — at least by end of August.
  • Do NOT submit any materials that their process does not specifically require. Only submit what they specifically ask for.
  • Do homework on your top 1 or 2 service academy priorities. Be able to explain what your top 1 or 2 job selections might be. Nominating authorities don't want to waste a nomination on candidates who haven't done their homework.
  • Letters of recommendation — start collecting early. Make sure recommenders actually KNOW you, your character, and your background. Seek a good mix of individuals from across a broad spectrum of your background.
  • For nomination interviews, dress up (coat and tie, dress/skirt) or wear a uniform if you have one. Everything they are looking for fits into three categories: motivation, strengths, and fit.
  • If you get an inappropriate question, use the opportunity to show grace, maturity, and poise. Be prepared with a strong opening response and try to answer even if uncomfortable.

After the Nomination & Getting 3Q'd

  • Receiving a nomination is just the beginning — WP receives about 3,000 nominations every admissions cycle. From this pool, competitive files are further processed through the 3Q process (Academic, Physical/CFA, and Medical qualifications).
  • About 2,000 files earn the final "Qualified" determination for the ~1,400 Offers that go out to fill a class of about 1,250.
  • Fully qualified or 3Q'd candidates who do not receive a nomination and/or offer of appointment remain competitive for the next admissions cycle. Going to college for one or two years, especially with ROTC participation, makes you more competitive.

Your Official WP Interview & Candidate Evaluation

  • DO NOT request an official interview from FF. FF members will reach out to schedule formal interviews when they feel the time is right.
  • FF will conduct the official interview when they see: (1) a majority of your file complete, (2) a "Passed" CFA on file, and/or (3) potential that you have a competitive file with a strong chance of earning an appointment.
  • Before the interview, send your FF Rep your resume (one page) and your latest high school transcript (unofficial).
  • Plan on at least 60 minutes to 1.5 hours and lots of Q&A. FF aims to keep interviews informal — always more of a meeting than an interview.
  • If attending still in high school, at least one parent/guardian is typically required to be present.
  • Never go to an interview "cold" or unprepared.

United States Military Academy – Interviewing Tips
Last updated August 2025

Interviewing / General

  • Be prepared for the few times you will need to explain yourself in formal interviews or informal conversations with adults in a position to help you. Have a good story for why West Point should offer you an appointment to a >$400,000 education and training. Prepare to explain what you can do for West Point, the Army, and your country — not the other way around.
  • Understand exactly what you are getting into. You are signing up for a highly rigorous education, physical and mental training, and a socialization process to prepare you for a career as a leader of character in the profession of arms. The first questions you will be asked is why you want to attend West Point to ultimately commission as an officer. Be prepared to answer:
    • Confidently without being arrogant or too boastful
    • Brilliantly while still showing humility
    • Succinctly while still being thorough
    • Patriotically without sounding too fanatical
  • Make notes and commit to memory well thought-out, extemporaneous answers to questions you should expect. West Point and the nominating authorities are primarily focused on a candidate's:
    • Character
    • Resilience
    • Desire to serve
    FF is primarily focused on your potential around: motivation, strengths, & fit for the West Point experience.
  • Be knowledgeable about challenges around you and why almost all situations require competent leadership to solve problems. Describe how you would be an asset to an organization. Be prepared with a well thought-out answer about how your leadership made a difference in a situation.
  • Research leadership and develop your own opinion. Be prepared to offer, define, and describe one to three characteristics of a good to great leader. Examples:
    • Empathetic — been in the shoes of those being led
    • Competent — well trained and has had the jobs of those being led
    • Emotionally intelligent — sensitivity and good listener
    • Committed / passionate (without being too overbearing)
    • Honest / integrity — doing the harder right instead of the easier wrong
    • Visionary / purposeful / gritty / resilient
  • Be prepared to discuss your experience and successes working in groups. The military is all about teamwork. Interviewers are wary of arrogance — be prepared to humbly discuss your experiences participating on and contributing to the success of teams. Your stories should be mostly about "we" not "I."

Interview Questions to Prepare For

Potential to Serve as a Leader of Character
  • Tell me about a time you faced an ethical dilemma. How did you respond? Would you make the same choice again?
  • Have you ever been faced with choosing a "harder right" over an "easier wrong"? How do you feel you responded?
  • Tell me about a time you made a mistake. What were your actions when you realized your mistake?
  • Have you ever encountered someone doing something unethical? How did you handle the situation?
Potential to Overcome Challenges and Adversity
  • Describe a time when you failed. What did you learn from this experience?
  • Describe a time when you had to or helped others overcome an inconvenience to success. Why did you do this and what did you learn?
  • What has been a deeply challenging experience for you? What did you feel was most critical to overcoming this challenge?
  • Tell me about a time when your actions or leadership made a difference in a challenging situation.
  • What do you consider to be the most important character traits in a leader or teammate when completing a challenging task?
Propensity for Military Service

This is where someone is trying to figure out your depth and breadth of understanding of what it means to serve.

  • How does West Point fit into your long-term goals?
  • What interests you about serving in the Army versus other career options (military or otherwise)?
  • Are you applying to other Service Academies or ROTC programs? If so, where does USMA rank in your preferences and why?
  • What field do you plan to major in and why?
  • What branch of the Army might you be most interested in and why?
  • Provide an example that proves that you are self-driven.
Other
  • What drives you through tough times?
  • What do you do to energize or recharge?
  • What drains you?

WP Interview Checklist
Field Force Interview Reference

Pre-Interview Preparation

  • Have printout of Interview Form as necessary; select a few formal questions to ask beforehand to weave into meeting; make candidate tell stories about themselves. Overall focus: determine Character, Resiliency, and Desire to Serve.
  • Print FF view of Candidate's Portal; review green and red checkmarks and encourage file completion by end of October. No reason they cannot complete WP file at same time as packets for nominating authorities.
  • Have latest available transcript(s) and resume — to get to know candidate beforehand. Help with major problems with resume.

Interview Flow

  • Focus on motivation, strengths, fit for the WP experience with overall eye on potential; be patient — many young people still develop a great deal in last several months of HS career.
  • Start with small talk. Why WP / service academy? Military family, veterans, history serving? Find out breadth and depth of understanding of what it means to serve.
  • College major? Why? Other schools applying to? Understand ROTC options? What is backup? What do they want to do in military? Discuss all service ROTCs and advantages.
  • Background discussion (transcript and resume); work in questions required from the formal evaluation form; determine academic fit and how candidate grew through HS/early college; how hard did they work on developing self? Where did they get themselves out of their comfort zone?
  • What energizes you? What drains you? And how do you recharge?
  • Nominating Process: advise on how to handle inappropriate questions; advise if candidate is eligible for other nomination sources other than MOC; remind to try to earn more than one nomination.
  • Weave in Q&A to include parents. Determine parent dynamic — helicopter, bulldozer? Who really wants to go to WP — the candidate or the parent?
  • Encourage candidate to finish file — do not wait until last minute.
  • Be able to write summary comments about Scholar, Leader, Athlete mix along with maturity, bearing, poise, grit, and STRAC-ness.

Section 4
HS & College Resume Preparation Tips
Last updated January 2025

These tips come from over 40 years helping candidates gain admission to West Point and other service academies, along with earning various ROTC scholarships, and over 30 years of experience working directly with college admissions officials while serving on numerous university scholarship and fellowship review boards. This should augment other resources, styles, and formats you may have already developed.

General Principles

  • Your resume is a giant business card, not an autobiography. Keep it brief to one page max, highlighting accomplishments and time spent in leadership roles, significant volunteer roles, or paid employment. Readers are looking for your motivation, strengths, and fit for their particular opportunity. Use your resume to drive the conversation.
  • Craft a lead — who are you and where are you going? Why should the reader spend more than 20 seconds with the rest of your document?
  • Review and update it regularly. Only show hometown, state, and zip code — no longer necessary to include house numbers and street names.
  • Fill in white space — it is not an MLA document, but make it neat and succinct.
  • Use Arial Narrow 12-point font for the body (11-point for bulleted items if needed). Calibri is also popular if you need to spread things out.
  • Do not be intimidated by the activities reflected on the examples — some details are included to show how to present specific parts of unique experiences.
  • High school only (12, 11, 10, 9 in this order) unless you participated in a unique club or activity that began before high school — in that case, include Mo/Yr activity began and ended.
  • If you have a significant number of items and cannot fit to one page, start with 2 pages then tailor separate one-page resumes to a particular purpose.
  • Include the number of hours spent on activities, especially for community/school projects with significant volunteering. Readers do not want to guess. Try to explain the impact your leadership made.
  • Spell out acronyms if space allows or if the organization is uncommon.
  • Keep lines of achievements to ONE LINE if possible.
  • List accomplishments in reverse chronological order — most recent event or grade first (12, 11, 10, 9 — NOT 9, 10, 11, 12). Also list from most important to least important.
  • Include activities, letters, or awards that will be earned (expected) throughout your senior year — note them as "(expected)".
  • Try NOT to include minor participation in anything if you were not an officer or did not hold some sort of leadership position — unless there was a significant service, volunteer, or teambuilding component.
  • If involved in an activity for 1 hour or less per week on a recurring basis — leave it off unless you have space.
  • Do not list hobbies or activities done in your free time unless there is a leadership or service component to it, or you are really good at it, or have received local, regional, statewide, or national recognition.

Education / Academics

Always list this category first!

  • Include school name and highlights from your transcript (GPA, test scores, etc.). If in college, list college background first.
  • Include EXACT value of your unweighted or weighted (or both) GPAs — do not round up. What you put down is the first thing easily checked against your transcript — it's the first test of "attention to detail."
  • Only SAT & ACT scores — no one cares about PSATs. Only break out parts/pieces if your results are in the 90+ percentiles.
  • Only list AP test scores or Concurrent Enrollment grades if the majority are 3 or higher (or As for CE courses).
  • List a particular level of AP honors recognition given by The College Board. Do not forget to include the year or grade you earned the distinction.
  • Include name of Concurrent Enrollment institution.
  • Include National Honor Society participation and any other science, language, or technical honor societies.
  • Include scholarship awards already received or honors colleges admitted to if significant.
  • Academic All-State recognitions should be included under the Athletic section.
  • Any and all student government or school club-related roles should be listed here.

Leadership (rare — use when you have enough distinct positions)

  • If you have enough significant, distinct leadership positions for at least 3–4 lines, create this section. Otherwise, forgo it and list prominent leadership roles throughout other sections.
  • This is a broad category — a student can hold leadership positions in virtually every endeavor: church, scouting, band, drama, chorus, academic clubs, cheer, drill team, Student Government, school newspaper/yearbook, junior ROTC, community advisory council, sports, etc.
  • For each leadership position, list the specific title and any leadership progression (Secretary → President, lower-level Scouting → Eagle Scout). Showing leadership progression is very important.
  • If you only held membership without a leadership position, leave it off or list under a catch-all "Club/Extracurricular/Community" section.

Athletics

  • List participation where you earned varsity letters first — list sports separately. Include sports where you were or are a team captain. Include academic all-state recognition for a particular sport.
  • If a member of a club or team not traditionally considered a regular sport (martial art, boxing, shooting, fitness, climbing, cycling, equestrian, drill team, cheer, dance, etc.), provide the same detail.
  • List school, district, regional, state, or national awards or honors separately.
  • Include anticipated senior year participation (expected) as the year goes by.
  • For summer sports camps, specify the particular month(s) attended — not just the year.
  • Include details related to youth coaching, mentoring, or officiating.

Community & Extracurriculars

  • List memberships in clubs, teams, volunteer, and work activities in which you only participated but did not list under Education or Athletics. Include a "summer abroad" (not a 1 or 2 week trip) or significant community work projects — include hours served.
  • If you have enough space and accomplishments, create a separate "AWARDS" section to highlight 3 or more. Otherwise, indicate special recognition in the most closely related activity section.
  • Include nontraditional fine, performing, and visual arts activities: yearbook, school paper, band, orchestra, chorus, dance, lead in a play, stage manager, art/set director, cheer, drill team, county/state fair recognition, 4H, FFA, DECA, FBLA, etc.
  • List any other significant accomplishments not mentioned elsewhere: certified Lifeguard, EMT, Firefighter, Police Cadet, Sport Officiating certifications, literary awards, or proven linguistic skills.
  • If you have space, include special interests that demonstrate a passion, mastery of a special skill, or interest not reflected elsewhere — especially if there is a leadership component.
  • Include any significant paid employment (full or part-time), especially if in a supervisory or managerial-type position. Denote whether work was summers only or during the academic year.
